# Ardtire Society Civic Digital Governance Platform
## Product Intent v2

## Document Status

---

# 1. Product Intent

The Ardtire Society Civic Digital Governance Platform exists to give the Society a serious, structured, digital civic environment in which people can move from public discovery, to authenticated participation, to reviewed membership, to meaningful involvement in constitutional and governance formation under explicit rules, defined roles, and durable institutional records.

This product is intended to make constitutional development, civic participation, proposal handling, deliberation, voting, publication, and governance memory operationally manageable and institutionally legitimate.

It is not merely a website, a forum, or a content portal. It is a civic process system: a digital platform for organized constitutional development and structured governance participation.

---

# 2. Core Vision

The long-term vision is for the platform to become the principal civic-digital environment of the Ardtire Society: the place where identity, membership, constitutional drafting, deliberation, proposal processing, decision-making, publication, and historical recordkeeping occur in one coherent and trusted system.

The platform should feel like a hybrid of:

- a constitutional convention workspace,
- a civic chamber,
- a controlled legislative drafting environment,
- an institutional archive,
- and a public-facing civic gateway.

The platform should embody:

- seriousness,
- procedural legitimacy,
- civic dignity,
- structured openness,
- institutional continuity,
- transparency where appropriate,
- privacy where necessary,
- and durable historical memory.

---

# 3. The Problem the Product Solves

Without a purpose-built platform, constitutional and civic formation becomes scattered across:

- chats,
- email threads,
- disconnected documents,
- ad hoc discussions,
- informal authority structures,
- and fragmented tools.

That creates predictable failures:

- no clear source of truth,
- unclear proposal status,
- weak version control,
- confused roles and permissions,
- inconsistent handling of membership and voting,
- poor transparency,
- weak auditability,
- fragile institutional memory,
- and reduced confidence in legitimacy.

This platform exists to solve that by making governance a first-class digital workflow.

---

# 4. Product Thesis

The platform should treat governance as explicit process, not informal conversation.

That means the core of the product should be built around modeled civic objects and workflows such as:

- accounts,
- user identities,
- membership applications,
- member statuses,
- proposals,
- amendments,
- discussion threads,
- revision histories,
- workflow states,
- voting windows,
- rule-governed outcomes,
- publication states,
- and authoritative records.

The platform should make it easy to answer questions such as:

- Who submitted this proposal?
- What status is it currently in?
- What is the latest revision?
- What changed between versions?
- Who is allowed to comment, sponsor, review, or vote?
- When did voting open and close?
- Which rule set governed this outcome?
- Was it adopted, rejected, returned for revision, withdrawn, or superseded?
- What text is currently authoritative?

If those questions cannot be answered clearly, the platform is not yet fulfilling its purpose.

---

# 5. Product Purpose

The platform exists to enable the Ardtire Society to:

1. gather people into a coherent civic community;
2. distinguish clearly between public access, authentication, membership, and authority;
3. allow approved members to participate in constitutional drafting and governance workflows;
4. structure proposal submission, revision, debate, and voting;
5. preserve a durable, intelligible record of how institutional text and decisions evolved;
6. support legitimacy through rule-governed process rather than improvised collaboration;
7. lay the digital foundation for future constitutional, parliamentary, and civic institutions.

---

# 6. Who the Product Is For

## 6.1 Primary users

### Public visitors
People discovering the Ardtire Society who need to understand its mission, principles, public materials, and participation pathway.

### Registered users
People who have platform accounts but are not yet approved members of the Society.

### Membership applicants
Registered users who have entered the Society’s membership review process.

### Approved Society members
People who may participate in formal constitutional and governance workflows according to defined permissions.

### Moderators, editors, reviewers, and administrators
Trusted platform operators who manage process integrity, content quality, membership handling, publication, safety, and administrative oversight.

## 6.2 Future extended users

The platform should also be capable of supporting future roles such as:

- committee members,
- officers,
- clerks,
- archivists,
- recordkeepers,
- governance facilitators,
- parliamentary moderators,
- and public readers of adopted materials.

---

# 7. Jobs To Be Done

## Public-facing jobs
- Help a new visitor understand what the Society is.
- Explain how one moves from observer to participant.
- Present official public materials in a credible, coherent way.

## Identity and membership jobs
- Allow a person to create an account.
- Allow a registered user to apply for membership.
- Allow the Society to review and decide on membership applications.
- Reflect a user’s current participation status clearly.

## Governance jobs
- Allow members to submit proposals.
- Allow members to discuss, revise, and evaluate proposals.
- Allow proposals to move through explicit procedural stages.
- Allow eligible members to vote during defined windows.
- Allow outcomes to be recorded according to rule sets.

## Institutional memory jobs
- Preserve proposal histories.
- Preserve revision histories.
- Preserve outcome records.
- Preserve authoritative text states over time.
- Make governance history legible and auditable.

# 8. Product Principles

## 8.1 Legitimacy over improvisation
The product should privilege traceable procedure over informal ambiguity.

## 8.2 Participation with structure
The product should encourage meaningful contribution, but inside governed workflows.

## 8.3 Clear distinction between identity, membership, and authority
Authentication is not membership.
Membership is not moderation.
Moderation is not constitutional authority.
Every status and role should be explicit.

## 8.4 Governance is a workflow
Important civic actions should move through defined states with explicit transitions, permissions, deadlines, and outcomes.

## 8.5 Transparency by design
Where appropriate, proposal states, procedures, histories, and outcomes should be visible and intelligible.

## 8.6 Historical durability
The platform must preserve how ideas, texts, and decisions evolved over time.

## 8.7 Authority must be explicit
The product must clearly distinguish:
- draft text,
- proposed text,
- in-review text,
- voting text,
- adopted text,
- superseded text,
- and archived text.

## 8.8 Institution before interface
The UI should serve the integrity of the civic process rather than reduce it to generic engagement mechanics.

## 8.9 Public trust through operational clarity
Users should be able to understand where they stand, what they can do, what happens next, and what rules apply.

## 8.10 Design for eventual constitutional seriousness
Even the MVP should be designed with future institutional gravity in mind.

# 9. What the Product Must Fundamentally Do

At a minimum, the platform must support five foundational layers.

## 9.1 Public layer
- Present the Society to the public.
- Publish public-facing mission, context, and process information.
- Explain the participation pathway.

## 9.2 Identity layer
- Support account registration and authentication.
- Support secure account lifecycle management.
- Associate actions with persistent user identities.

## 9.3 Membership layer
- Support membership application, review, decision, and status tracking.
- Distinguish clearly between registered users and approved members.

## 9.4 Governance workflow layer
- Support proposal submission.
- Support deliberation and revision.
- Support stage-based workflow transitions.
- Support formal voting windows.
- Support rule-based outcomes and status changes.
- Preserve version history and rationale.

## 9.5 Publication and archive layer
- Publish authoritative drafting state.
- Publish relevant adopted materials.
- Preserve historic proposals, revisions, discussions, and outcomes.
- Maintain durable institutional memory.

# 10. Recommended MVP Intent

The MVP should focus on the smallest coherent civic loop that proves the platform’s purpose.

## Foundational loop
1. A visitor discovers the Society.
2. The visitor registers an account.
3. The registered user submits a membership application.
4. The application is reviewed by authorized operators.
5. Approved applicants become members.
6. Members gain access to governance participation features.
7. Members submit proposals.
8. Members discuss and revise proposals.
9. Proposals enter voting windows.
10. Outcomes are recorded.
11. The authoritative drafting state is updated or preserved accordingly.

If this loop works well, the platform is already performing its core institutional function.

# 11. MVP Capability Boundaries

## In scope for MVP
- Public site pages
- Account registration and login
- User dashboard
- Membership application flow
- Application review flow
- Member-only proposal submission
- Proposal discussion
- Proposal revision/versioning
- Proposal workflow states
- Voting windows and vote recording
- Outcome recording
- Authoritative draft publication
- Basic archives and history
- Role-based permissions
- Admin moderation tools
- Notification basics
- Audit trail basics

## Deliberately out of scope for the earliest version
- Complex parliamentary procedure beyond what is necessary for constitutional drafting
- Full committee system
- Live real-time floor procedures
- Advanced delegate representation models
- External public referenda
- Deep gamification or social-network-style engagement systems
- Broad civic super-app functionality unrelated to the constitutional process
- Overbuilt ceremony features before core governance workflows work reliably

# 12. Non-Goals

The platform is not primarily intended to be:

- a generic social network,
- an unstructured discussion forum,
- a casual community chat application,
- a simple CMS-only marketing site,
- a file dump,
- or a tool that substitutes vague engagement for clear governance.

Discussion, publishing, and community features may exist, but they should remain in service of institutional governance workflows.

# 14. Product Success Definition

The product succeeds when the Society actually uses it as the trusted place where constitutional life is conducted rather than merely described.

That means:

- visitors understand the Society and its participation pathway;
- users can register and track their status;
- membership decisions are handled coherently;
- members can submit and evaluate proposals;
- proposals move through understandable states;
- votes are rule-governed and recorded;
- authoritative drafting state is visible;
- administrators can manage workflows without excessive manual overhead;
- and the Society gains a reliable digital institutional memory.

# 15. Success Metrics

## 15.1 Adoption metrics
- number of registered users,
- number of membership applications started,
- number of applications completed,
- member activation rate after approval,
- percentage of approved members who engage in governance workflows.

## 15.2 Governance throughput metrics
- number of proposals submitted,
- number of proposals discussed,
- number of proposals revised,
- number of proposals brought to vote,
- average cycle time from submission to resolution.

## 15.3 Process integrity metrics
- percentage of proposals with complete history,
- percentage of votes with complete auditability,
- percentage of outcomes tied to explicit rule versions,
- number of unresolved ambiguous states,
- moderation/admin time required per workflow.

## 15.4 Institutional trust metrics
- clarity of user status,
- clarity of proposal status,
- reduction in off-platform process confusion,
- operator confidence in records,
- member confidence in fairness and legitimacy.

# 16. Design Implications

The product intent implies several design consequences.

## 16.1 Strong role visibility
Users should always be able to tell:
- who they are in the system,
- what permissions they currently have,
- what status their membership holds,
- and what actions are available to them.

## 16.2 State visibility
Every governance object should present its current status clearly.

## 16.3 Version visibility
Users must be able to see that text changes over time and know which version is authoritative.

## 16.4 Serious tone
The interface should convey institutional credibility rather than casual social-media energy.

## 16.5 Low ambiguity
Users should not have to guess what happens next in a proposal, application, or vote workflow.

## 16.6 First-class history
History is not an afterthought. It is part of the core product.

---

# 17. Operating Assumptions

This product intent assumes the following:

1. Public visitors may register for platform accounts without automatically becoming Society members.
2. Membership requires an application and approval process.
3. Approved members may participate in constitutional drafting.
4. Constitutional drafting is a hybrid process: open enough for contribution, structured enough for legitimacy.
5. Proposal workflows will be explicit rather than ad hoc.
6. Voting outcomes will be governed by defined rules.
7. The platform must preserve durable records of decisions and text evolution.

These assumptions should remain stable unless intentionally revised by product/governance decision.
